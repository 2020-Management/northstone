Most enterprise software is bought because someone decided it would help. It is renewed for as long as that decision still looks good — and cut the moment a budget cycle turns. Governance, risk and compliance software is different in kind. It is bought because an organisation has no lawful alternative, and renewed because removing it would re-open an obligation the organisation is required to meet.

That distinction is the whole investment case.

## Mandated demand behaves differently

A discretionary budget flexes with sentiment. A mandated one does not. When a control is required by regulation, the spend that satisfies it sits below the line where cost-cutting usually operates — closer to audit fees and insurance than to the marketing stack. It is defended in good years and in bad.

> The best software budget to own is the one a customer cannot responsibly cancel.

## Retention compounds into pricing power

Software that becomes the system of record for a regulated process accumulates two things over time: data the organisation cannot easily move, and workflows the organisation has built its operations around. Both raise switching costs. Both make a renewal conversation about continuity rather than price — which is exactly the conversation a durable software business wants to have.

- Net revenue retention that holds through downturns.
- Expansion as new obligations layer onto the same platform.
- Pricing that tracks the value of the risk being managed, not the cost of the code.

We look for companies sitting on top of mandated processes, where the software has quietly become infrastructure. That is where the most defensible budgets in the enterprise are spent — and where ownership compounds.
