Ask a chief financial officer to name the line items that survive a hostile budget review untouched, and the answer is consistent across industries: external audit fees, the directors-and-officers premium, the legal reserve, the cost of keeping a banking licence current. None is bought for a return. Each is the price of remaining a going concern, and each is the last thing a finance function cuts, because cutting it does not save money so much as convert a known cost into an unbounded one.

The argument we want to make about governance, risk and compliance software is narrow. Over the past three years the spend that satisfies regulatory controls has migrated out of the discretionary technology budget, where it competed with collaboration tools and analytics platforms, and into that protected tier. It is no longer evaluated as software. It is evaluated as the operating cost of a supervised obligation. Where a budget sits in the cost hierarchy determines how it behaves under pressure, and this one now sits in the most defended part of the enterprise.

## What moved the line item

The migration was not a matter of taste. Three regulatory developments, each real and each recent, reclassified the underlying activity from a managerial preference into a duty owed to a supervisor.

The first relocated accountability onto named individuals. Under the EU Digital Operational Resilience Act — Regulation (EU) 2022/2554, in application since 17 January 2025 — the management body of a financial entity must, under Article 5(2), define, approve, oversee and bear responsibility for the ICT risk-management framework. NIS2, the parallel cybersecurity directive (Directive (EU) 2022/2555), goes further: Article 20 makes management bodies approve and supervise cybersecurity measures and holds them liable for the entity's infringements, and for essential entities Article 32(5) lets a competent authority temporarily bar a chief executive from managerial functions. When the obligation attaches to a person rather than a department, the spend that discharges it stops being departmental.

The second converted intent into evidence on a regulator's timetable. DORA's incident regime, set out in the reporting technical standard (Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2025/301), requires an initial notification of a major incident within four hours of its classification as major and no later than twenty-four hours from awareness, an intermediate report within seventy-two hours of that initial notification, and a final report within a month of the intermediate report. NIS2 imposes a comparable cascade under Article 23(4): a twenty-four-hour early warning, a seventy-two-hour notification, and a final report within a month of that notification. Obligations expressed in hours cannot be met by goodwill and a shared drive. They presuppose tooling already running before the incident occurs.

The third is enforceable scale. NIS2 sets fine ceilings of at least ten million euro or two per cent of worldwide annual turnover for essential entities under Article 34(4), and at least seven million euro or 1.4 per cent for important entities under Article 34(5), whichever is higher. The EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689) reaches thirty-five million euro or seven per cent of turnover for the prohibited-practice breaches in Article 99(3). In the United States, the SEC's 2023 rules — effective 5 September 2023 — added Regulation S-K Item 106, requiring every registrant to describe in its Form 10-K its processes, if any, for assessing, identifying and managing material cybersecurity risk, and how the board oversees it. The rule does not mandate the controls; it mandates the disclosure. But it makes the absence of a process a fact a board must state in its annual report, and no board wants that as its answer.

> A budget defended by the audit committee behaves nothing like a budget defended by a product owner, and GRC spend has changed which committee it answers to.

## The economics that follow from the reclassification

Once spend belongs to the protected tier, its unit economics improve for reasons that have little to do with the product and everything to do with the buyer's incentives.

- Renewal ceases to be a value judgement and becomes a continuity decision, because the alternative to renewing is re-opening a supervised gap rather than reverting to a spreadsheet.
- Pricing references the magnitude of the exposure being managed — a turnover-percentage fine, a personal liability, a disclosure obligation — not the marginal cost of the software, which is why these budgets tolerate escalation that discretionary tools cannot.
- New regimes do not replace the old; DORA, NIS2 and the AI Act each layer obligations onto the same governance function, so the protected line item widens with every legislative cycle rather than churning.

The market data, used carefully, is consistent. Estimates of the enterprise GRC software market diverge widely because houses draw the boundary differently, so a single headline figure would mislead. But the growth signal is directionally agreed: MarketsandMarkets projects the segment expanding from 20.56 billion dollars in 2025 to 39.99 billion by 2030, a compound annual rate of 14.2 per cent, with analyst commentary attributing the demand specifically to the SEC's cyber rules, data-privacy regimes and ESG mandates. Double-digit growth in a category whose customers are buying obligation rather than advantage is the quantitative shadow of the reclassification we have described.

We underwrite businesses on the assumption that their revenue is as durable as the reason their customers pay for it. The most durable reason in enterprise software today is that a named executive is personally answerable to a regulator, and the software is the evidence of the answer. That is the budget we want to own.
