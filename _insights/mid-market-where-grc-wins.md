Most arguments for the mid-market begin with sentiment: these buyers are nimble, the enterprise is saturated, the long tail too small to bother with. None of that explains why the segment should be structurally attractive rather than merely available. The better explanation is that Europe's regulators have drawn the addressable market themselves. The newest cyber and resilience regimes set their scope by the size of the enterprise, and the line they draw falls almost exactly across firms with the most obligation per unit of capacity to meet it.

## The statute draws the boundary

Consider how NIS2 — Directive (EU) 2022/2555, with national transposition due by 17 October 2024 and application from 18 October 2024 — defines who is in scope. Article 2(1) applies the Directive to entities that qualify as medium-sized enterprises, or exceed those ceilings, and operate in one of its listed sectors. Medium-sized, by the Annex to Commission Recommendation 2003/361/EC, means broadly 50 or more employees or more than EUR 10 million in turnover or balance sheet. The effect is a deliberate size-cap: micro and most small firms fall outside the general rule, while medium and large enterprises across the Commission's eighteen sectors are pulled in. The obligation begins, by design, at the point a firm crosses into the mid-market.

What lands on those firms is not light. Article 20(1) makes management bodies responsible for approving cybersecurity risk-management measures and overseeing their implementation, and holds them liable for the entity's infringements. Article 23(4) sets a staged cadence: an early warning within 24 hours and a fuller notification within 72 hours, each measured from awareness of a significant incident, then a final report within one month of that notification. A large bank has a security operations centre to absorb this. A 200-person logistics or chemicals business in Annex I or II does not.

DORA reaches in the same direction from the financial side. Regulation (EU) 2022/2554 has applied since 17 January 2025 and binds roughly twenty-one categories of financial entity under Article 2(1), from payment institutions to crowdfunding service providers. Its Article 16 simplified framework eases the burden for the smallest entities through proportionality rather than exemption — core duties such as incident reporting still apply. The advanced obligations sit above them: a register of information on every ICT third-party arrangement under Article 28(3), and threat-led penetration testing at least every three years under Article 26 for entities a competent authority deems significant. A mid-sized investment firm carries the full architecture of obligation with a fraction of a tier-one bank's resilience function.

> The largest firms can hire their way to compliance and the smallest are largely spared; only the middle is both obliged and outmatched.

## Why the economics, not just the need, favour this segment

Need alone does not make a good segment to sell into. The mid-market is attractive because its buying behaviour suits how this software is best built and sold. The reasoning is structural, not anecdotal.

A regulated mid-market buyer has a defined trigger — a transposition deadline, an audit finding, a first incident that exposes the 72-hour clock — and a short chain of approval to satisfy it. That compresses the sales cycle relative to the enterprise, where the same purchase passes through procurement, security architecture and legal first. It also shapes deployment: a firm without a dozen specialists cannot absorb a twelve-month rollout, so it selects for software that stands up in weeks and is operable by generalists. That constraint, often treated as a limitation, is in fact a filter that rewards vendors who make capability legible to non-specialists.

Retention follows from the same scarcity of staff. When a mid-market firm encodes its incident workflow and its third-party register into a tool, the people who would manage a migration are the same people running compliance day to day, and there are too few of them to spare. The obligation does not lapse, the headcount to re-platform does not exist, and renewal becomes the path of least resistance. As the firm grows past the next threshold, or another regime layers on, it reaches for an adjacent module from a provider it already trusts rather than run a fresh evaluation.

- The regulatory size-cap defines a large, statutorily obliged population the enterprise suites were never priced to reach.
- Shorter approval chains and a concrete deadline compress the sales cycle.
- Lean teams force fast, generalist-operable deployment, and the scarcity of specialists that drives the purchase also suppresses churn.

The enterprise solved its problem with bespoke programmes a decade ago, and the smallest firms are mostly left alone. The companies we find most interesting meet the regulated middle on its own terms — quick to deploy, priced for a firm without a compliance department, and built to widen as the obligations do. That is not a niche left over after the enterprise was served. It is the segment the regulation was written around.
