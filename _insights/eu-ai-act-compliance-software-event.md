Every major regulation arrives wrapped in the language of its subject — data, cyber, financial resilience, and now artificial intelligence. Underneath that language, the demand it creates is almost always the same: organisations must document what they do, prove they have controls in place and produce evidence on request. The EU AI Act is no exception. For all the novelty of its subject, it is, operationally, a compliance-software event.

## Novel subject, familiar mechanics

The Act asks providers and deployers of high-risk AI systems to maintain technical documentation, manage risk across the lifecycle, keep records and demonstrate human oversight. None of that is met by intention. It is met by a system that captures the work as it happens and can reproduce the evidence later — the same shape of tooling that DORA, NIS2 and GDPR before it all required.

> A new domain of regulation almost always becomes a new module in the GRC stack.

## Why this widens the surface

For GRC software, the AI Act does not replace existing demand — it layers on top of it. The organisations that already manage cyber and data-protection obligations now have one more regime to evidence, ideally in the same place. That is exactly how the addressable need for these platforms compounds.

- Documentation and lifecycle risk management for high-risk systems.
- Record-keeping and oversight that must be demonstrable, not asserted.
- A new obligation that maps naturally onto an existing system of record.

The companies building toward this will not treat AI governance as a standalone product for long. They will treat it as another capability in a platform that already carries the rest of an organisation's obligations. That is the pattern we invest behind.
