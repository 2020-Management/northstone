The GRC software market did not grow up. It accreted. Each new obligation produced a new tool — one for vendor risk, one for policy management, one for control testing, one for incident reporting — and most organisations ended up running several of them at once, stitched together by spreadsheets and the people who maintain them. The result is a market of hundreds of capable point solutions and very few platforms.

That fragmentation is not a flaw to route around. It is the opportunity.

## Why consolidation creates value here

When obligations overlap, the tools that serve them duplicate data, controls and effort. A platform that unifies them removes that duplication and turns a collection of subscriptions into a single source of truth. The buyer gets one place to demonstrate compliance; the vendor gets a wider, stickier footprint and a natural path to expand.

> Fragmentation is what a buy-and-build strategy is for: many good products, one missing platform.

## The shape of a build

Buy-and-build only works when the pieces actually fit. We look for a coherent data model the acquisitions can share, a commercial motion that lets one module lead to the next, and an integration discipline that turns acquired products into parts of a suite rather than a portfolio of logos.

- A modular architecture that lets customers adopt one capability and expand.
- A shared system of record so every module makes the others more useful.
- A commercial engine that cross-sells across the installed base.

The companies that get this right do more than roll up a fragmented market — they define the category that replaces it. That is the kind of platform Northstone sets out to build.
