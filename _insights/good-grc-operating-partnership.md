Most private equity ownership of a software company fails not through neglect but through diligence applied evenly to everything, and therefore decisively to nothing. A governance, risk and compliance business punishes that error harder than most, because its trajectory is fixed early by a few choices that are close to irreversible. Once a platform has committed to a regime, joined its modules a particular way and priced itself into a buyer's budget, those decisions calcify into its shape. The owner's contribution is to read, before they harden, the choices that cannot easily be unmade.

What makes GRC unusual is that the right answers are not matters of taste; they are legible in the structure of the regulations themselves. An owner who can read that structure has an edge no operating cadence supplies.

## Reading the obligation before committing to it

A GRC platform cannot serve every regime at once, and the regime it leads with determines who it sells to, how it prices and how defensible it becomes. Consider DORA. The Digital Operational Resilience Act, Regulation (EU) 2022/2554, has applied to financial entities since 17 January 2025, and its demands are not equal in software terms. The register of information required under Article 28(3) — a complete, maintained record of every contractual arrangement for ICT services from third-party providers, submitted to competent authorities — is a structured, recurring, audit-grade obligation. It is the kind of thing an organisation cannot keep in a spreadsheet for long without exposure, and the kind a vendor can own.

Incident reporting under the same regime is just as concrete but shaped differently. The technical standard, Commission Delegated Regulation (EU) 2025/301, sets an initial notification within four hours of an incident being classified as major, an intermediate report within 72 hours of that notification, and a final report within a month of the intermediate report. A platform built around the register and one built around the reporting clock are different companies, with different buyers and different competitors. That is an owner-level choice wearing the costume of a roadmap item, near-impossible to reverse once a sales motion has formed around it.

> The regime a platform leads with is not a roadmap detail; it is the company the business is choosing to become.

The same reading applies across regimes. NIS2 reserves its heavier supervisory regime, and its sharper enforcement, for essential entities rather than important ones, and a vendor that knows which side of that line its buyers sit on positions accordingly. The discipline is not enumerating regulations but identifying which obligation creates the most durable software surface and serving it first.

## Architecture and price, settled while they are still cheap

Obligations arrive at an organisation one at a time, over years, yet they overlap heavily underneath: the same control evidences several of them, the same third-party register feeds resilience, procurement and incident response. Whether the modules share a common spine or merely sit beside one another is decided early and expensive to undo. The question must be forced before the second module ships, when it can still be answered cheaply.

Price is the other early commitment that hardens. Most software is sold by consumption — seats, usage, volume. A compliance platform is not selling capacity; it is selling the management of a liability. The buyer's true comparison is not the cost of another login but the cost of the exposure the software removes: a board that cannot evidence the oversight DORA's Article 5 places squarely on the management body, a fine ceiling that under NIS2 runs to at least 2% of worldwide turnover for essential entities. Seat-based pricing leaves most of that value on the table, because the value scales with the magnitude of the risk managed, not the headcount touching the tool. Once a price has anchored a buyer's expectations, it rarely moves far, so it should be set deliberately rather than inherited from a sales team with no reason to think about it.

## Where the edge actually lies

- Lead with the obligation that creates the most durable software surface, then sequence the rest.
- Settle the shared data architecture before the second module, not after the fifth.
- Price to the magnitude of risk managed, not the number of seats.
- Make the senior scaling hires on the company's clock, neither early nor late.

These are the commitments that resist correction later, so they are the ones worth getting right while the cost of doing so is still low. Everything downstream — feature priorities within a settled architecture, the daily operating rhythm, hiring below the executive tier — is recoverable, and belongs to the operators. An owner who reaches into the recoverable decisions buys friction without buying trajectory. The craft is to spend judgement where it cannot be unspent, and to trust the people running the business with the rest.
